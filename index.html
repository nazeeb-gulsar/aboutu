This is an interesting geometric and conceptual problem! Let's break it down step by step. ### Given: - You start at the center of 10 concentric circles. - You move to the circumference of the first circle and travel a distance equal to the radius of that circle along the arc in a clockwise direction. - You then "jump" to the second circle and move along its arc by a distance equal to the second radius, and so on for subsequent circles. - You need to determine which circle (the \(n\)th circle) you will be on when you first reach the **positive quadrant**. ### Assumptions: 1. **Positive quadrant** means the portion of the circle where both the x and y coordinates are positive. 2. The angle covered when you travel along the circumference is proportional to the radius. ### Plan: - Each time you move along a circle's circumference by a distance equal to its radius, you're covering a certain angle. The angle covered depends on the ratio of the arc length traveled to the total circumference. - For the \(n\)th circle with radius \(r_n\), the circumference is \(C_n = 2\pi r_n\), and you travel a distance \(r_n\). Therefore, the angle covered (\(\theta_n\)) in radians is: \[ \theta_n = \frac{r_n}{2\pi r_n} \times 2\pi = 1 \text{ radian} \] So, each time you move along the arc, you cover **1 radian** of the circle’s total \(2\pi\) radians. To convert this to degrees: \(1 \text{ radian} \approx 57.3^\circ\). ### General Approach: 1. After moving on the first circle, you’ll have rotated \(57.3^\circ\) clockwise. 2. After the second circle, you’ll have added another \(57.3^\circ\), resulting in a total rotation of \(2 \times 57.3^\circ = 114.6^\circ\). 3. Continue this process for the \(n\)th circle. ### To Find: You need to reach the **positive quadrant**, which is between \(0^\circ\) and \(90^\circ\). This means the total angle of rotation after \(n\) moves should land in this range. ### Calculation: The total angle after \(n\) circles is: \[ \theta_{\text{total}} = n \times 57.3^\circ \] Now, you need to figure out the smallest \(n\) such that: \[ \theta_{\text{total}} \mod 360^\circ \in [0^\circ, 90^\circ] \] This means the total angle (mod 360°) should land in the first quadrant. #### Step-by-step: - \(n = 1\): \(\theta = 57.3^\circ\) (Still in the first quadrant) - \(n = 2\): \(\theta = 114.6^\circ\) (Second quadrant) - \(n = 3\): \(\theta = 171.9^\circ\) (Second quadrant) - \(n = 4\): \(\theta = 229.2^\circ\) (Third quadrant) - \(n = 5\): \(\theta = 286.5^\circ\) (Fourth quadrant) - \(n = 6\): \(\theta = 343.8^\circ\) (Fourth quadrant, approaching the first quadrant) - \(n = 7\): \(\theta = 401.1^\circ \mod 360^\circ = 41.1^\circ\) At \(n = 7\), you reach an angle of \(41.1^\circ\), which is in the positive quadrant. ### Conclusion: You will first reach the positive quadrant when you are on the **7th circle**.
